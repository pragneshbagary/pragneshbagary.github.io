Alignment fingerprints: a per-dimension decomposition of reward alignment

A single alignment score can tell a reward designer that something is wrong, but not what. This post decomposes the Trajectory Alignment Coefficient into exact per-dimension Shapley attributions, then maps where the resulting diagnostic localizes reward misspecification and where it provably cannot.

My previous post replicated Soft-TAC on LunarLander and ended with an open question. The Trajectory Alignment Coefficient (TAC) measures how well a reward function agrees with a human's preferences, and it reports that agreement as a single number: the Kendall tau-b correlation between the trajectory rankings the reward induces and the rankings the human provides. The scalar form is what makes TAC practical as a selection criterion and as a training loss, but it limits its value as a diagnostic. A designer who sees \(\mathrm{TAC} = 0.6\) learns that the reward disagrees with the human somewhere, but not where. Since the reward in my setup is a vector of eight interpretable feature weights, the natural question is whether the agreement computation can be attributed to individual dimensions: an alignment fingerprint rather than an alignment score.

This post develops that decomposition and evaluates it against a known ground truth. Some of what follows is positive and some of it is negative, and I have tried to give both the same weight: the method localizes sign errors reliably, it stops working past a measurable correlation boundary, and there is a class of errors it cannot see at all.

Background and setup

All experiments run on the pipeline from the replication post. Each LunarLander trajectory is summarized by a feature vector \(\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{8}\), the per-episode sum of eight per-step features: progress toward the pad, change in speed, change in tilt, leg contact, side-engine and main-engine firing, and terminal crash and safe-landing indicators. A fixed hidden weight vector

\[ w^{*} = (-2,\ -0.5,\ -1,\ 1,\ -0.1,\ -0.1,\ -5,\ 5) \]

acts as the human: it scores each trajectory as \(w^{*\top}\Phi\) and labels all 2775 pairs of 75 trajectories according to which one scores higher.

A candidate reward \(w \in \mathbb{R}^{8}\) induces its own preference on each pair, \(s_{ij}(w) = \operatorname{sign}\big(w^{\top}(\Phi_i - \Phi_j)\big)\), and its TAC is the Kendall tau-b agreement between those induced preferences and the labels \(y\). With \(P\) concordant and \(Q\) discordant pairs among the pairs untied on both sides,

\[ \mathrm{TAC}(w) \;=\; \frac{P - Q}{\sqrt{M_s \, M_y}}, \]

where \(M_s\) and \(M_y\) count the pairs left untied by the reward and by the labels respectively. A value of 1 means the reward ranks every pair the way the human does; 0 means no association.

Because \(w^{*}\) is known, every diagnostic in this post can be scored against ground truth. This is a validation the original TAC studies could not perform, since they observed only the human's preferences, never the underlying weights. The cost of the design is that the labeler is exactly linear in the features, a simplification that real human preferences will not satisfy. To control for dataset-specific artifacts, every headline number was reproduced on three independently regenerated trajectory pools, each built from retrained agents and fresh rollouts.

A univariate baseline fails under feature correlation

The simplest candidate decomposition is univariate: compute \(u_k = \tau_b\big(\operatorname{sign}(\Delta\Phi_k),\, y\big)\), the agreement between feature \(k\) alone and the human's preference labels. It is cheap, it needs no candidate reward, and on this data it gets a sign backwards.

On raw features, the crash indicator receives a univariate tau of +0.36, which reads as crashing being preferred. The true \(w^{*}\) assigns crash a weight of -5, the strongest penalty in the vector. The cause is feature correlation. Trajectories that crash end early and therefore fire the main engine less (corr(crash, main_eng) = -0.61 on this pool); main engine usage is genuinely dispreferred, and the crash feature inherits credit through that correlation. This is the standard failure mode of univariate screening, and it matters here because trajectory features are correlated in almost any realistic dataset. A usable decomposition has to handle this case.

Shapley attribution over reward dimensions

The Shapley value (Shapley, 1953) is cooperative game theory's answer to a credit-assignment question: when a set of players jointly produces a payoff, how should the payoff be divided among them? It gives each player the average of their marginal contributions over every order in which the players could join, and it is the unique division satisfying a short list of fairness axioms. The same construction is widely used in machine learning to attribute a model's prediction to its input features, most visibly in SHAP (Lundberg and Lee, 2017). For readers new to it, the Wikipedia article covers the definition and axioms, and the Shapley-value chapter of Christoph Molnar's Interpretable Machine Learning is a good tutorial on the attribution use case. Here, the players are the reward's dimensions and the payoff is its TAC.

Concretely, for a candidate reward \(w\) with \(d\) dimensions, define the value of a coalition \(S \subseteq \{1, \dots, d\}\) as \(v(S) = \mathrm{TAC}(w \odot \mathbf{1}_S)\): the TAC of the reward with every component outside \(S\) set to zero, and \(v(\emptyset) = 0\). The fingerprint entry for dimension \(k\) is its Shapley value,

\[ F_k(w) \;=\; \sum_{S \,\subseteq\, \{1,\dots,d\} \setminus \{k\}} \frac{|S|!\,(d - |S| - 1)!}{d!}\, \Big( v(S \cup \{k\}) - v(S) \Big). \]

With \(d = 8\) there are only \(2^{8} = 256\) coalitions, so the attribution is computed exactly, with no sampling, in milliseconds.

The construction inherits two of the standard Shapley axioms. Efficiency means the values sum exactly to the aggregate score, \(\sum_{k=1}^{d} F_k(w) = \mathrm{TAC}(w)\), so the fingerprint is a true decomposition of the metric rather than a separate heuristic sitting next to it. The null-player axiom means a dimension with zero weight, which never changes the value of any coalition, gets an attribution of exactly zero. Keep that second one in mind: it is the reason, later in the post, that the fingerprint turns out to be blind to omitted reward terms.

The fingerprint supports a simple decision rule. A dimension with a clearly negative \(F_k\) is actively pushing the reward's rankings away from the human's, and in a hand-designed reward that almost always indicates a sign error. On the crash confound described above, the fingerprint assigns crash the correct negative attribution that univariate screening gets backwards.

The effect of feature scale

The replication post documented how feature scale distorted one of its headline results. The same issue appeared here within the first hour of experiments, and it affects everything downstream.

On raw features, the two engine-usage features are per-episode firing counts in the hundreds, while crash and safe landing are binary flags, so the engine terms dominate the labeling score. The fingerprint of \(w^{*}\) evaluated against its own labels makes this concrete: the two engine dimensions carry 0.82 of the total attribution of 1.0 (0.51 and 0.31). In other words, the raw-feature labeler is mostly ranking trajectories by fuel usage, regardless of what \(w^{*}\) was meant to express. The scalar TAC of 1.0 cannot reveal this; the decomposition does. After dividing each feature by its standard deviation, the attribution mass moves to crash, progress, and safe landing, which matches the intent of the weights. All results below use standardized features, and the unscaled numbers are reported where they mark the resolution limit of the raw data.

Localizing sign errors

The core experiment: take \(w^{*}\), flip the sign of one dimension, and check whether the flipped dimension receives the most negative fingerprint value. This simulates the most common way a hand-designed reward goes wrong, a term built on the right feature with the wrong direction.

On standardized features, the fingerprint identifies the flipped dimension in 8 of 8 cases. The result reproduces on all three trajectory pools and remains stable down to preference budgets of one to two hundred pairs, against the 148 pairs the original TAC user study collected from humans. The most informative case is the main engine. Flipping main_eng moves the scalar TAC from 1.0 to 0.997, a change an aggregate metric cannot register, yet the fingerprint still places the flipped dimension at the minimum. Most cases are not marginal: flipping safe_land gives it an attribution of -0.465 while the next-lowest dimension remains positive.

On raw features, the result drops to 5 of 8, and the failures are systematic rather than random. Every pool fails on the same three dimensions (change in speed, change in tilt, crash), and each failed case is misattributed to another member of that same trio. These are exactly the dimensions the raw-feature labels barely encode once the engine terms absorb the ranking. I interpret this as a resolution limit of the preference data rather than of the attribution method, and feature standardization is what removes it.

Correlation as a controlled variable

In LunarLander, the feature correlations are fixed by the physics and by the behavior of the DQN checkpoints. To test the confound explanation directly, I built a 9x9 gridworld in which a mixing fraction \(m\) controls what share of item-B tiles are placed on top of item-A tiles. The ground-truth reward values A and assigns B a weight of exactly zero, so B is worthless to the human. A spurious candidate reward pays +2 for B anyway, and the question is whether any diagnostic can detect that.

Sweeping \(m\) from 0 to 1 turns the crash confound into a curve, with five generation seeds per point. Univariate credit to the worthless feature rises monotonically from -0.11 to +0.58, and at full co-location the A and B feature columns are identical, so no per-feature statistic could separate them even in principle. The fingerprint behaves differently. B's Shapley value starts clearly negative, correctly flagging the misspecification, and crosses zero as \(\mathrm{corr}(A, B)\) passes from roughly 0.2 to 0.6. Over the same sweep, the scalar TAC of the spurious reward stays between 0.82 and 0.90, never indicating a problem.

A synthetic sweep with Gaussian features makes the boundary precise and adds the property I consider most useful: the detection threshold scales with the severity of the misspecification. A spurious weight of 0.5 stops being detectable above roughly \(\rho \approx 0.4\), a weight of 2 crosses near \(\rho \approx 0.6\), and a weight of 4 remains detectable to roughly \(\rho \approx 0.8\). More damaging errors stay visible at higher correlation, which is the right way around: the mistakes that would hurt the most are the hardest ones to hide. Sign-flip localization on the genuinely valued dimensions stays perfect at every correlation level in both environments, so what is lost at high correlation is specifically the ability to flag an extra term on a correlated worthless feature. Past that boundary, the failure is not a weakness of the fingerprint: rewards that trade weight between A and B induce nearly identical rankings, so the preference data itself cannot distinguish them, and no diagnostic computed from the same preferences could.

Above the boundary, the failure is silent. B's attribution drifts to small positive values as its credit is absorbed by the correlated informative feature, so a practitioner sees a clean fingerprint rather than an alarm on the wrong dimension. This is the opposite of univariate screening, which fails loudly in the wrong direction, and it sets the rule for using this tool in practice: an unremarkable fingerprint over highly correlated features is weak evidence of alignment, not strong evidence.

Robustness to label noise

Flipping each preference label independently with probability \(p\) leaves the localization results essentially unchanged through \(p = 0.3\). At \(p = 0.5\), where the labels carry no information, localization collapses to chance, which is the negative control behaving as it should. That table is less informative than it looks, though. Uniform label flipping scales the tau of every coalition by \((1 - 2p)\) in expectation, so all Shapley values shrink by the same factor and the ordering of the minimum is preserved until finite-sample noise dominates. I verified this scaling directly: the observed shrinkage matches \((1 - 2p)\) to three decimal places at every noise level. This robustness therefore belongs to any rank-based method, not to the fingerprint specifically, and the argument does not cover noise concentrated on near-tie pairs, which is a more realistic model of human labeling error.

What the fingerprint cannot detect

The null-player property has a sharp consequence. If the designer omits a term the human cares about, that dimension's Shapley value is exactly zero by construction. The fingerprint can identify a term pointing in the wrong direction; it cannot, even in principle, identify a term that is missing.

I tested a complementary heuristic for omissions: flag the dimension with the largest gap between its univariate alignment and its Shapley contribution, on the reasoning that an omitted feature predicts the human's preferences while contributing nothing to the reward's agreement. The heuristic does not work. Its gap score is dominated by each dimension's baseline value, which is present even when nothing is omitted, so it flags nearly the same dimension regardless of which one was zeroed, and its apparent successes are just that constant default happening to land on the right answer. Omission detection remains unsolved in this setup, not partially solved. This failure is also silent: an omitted term produces no anomaly at all, so a clean fingerprint is never evidence that the feature set is complete.

The fingerprint in a repair loop

The original TAC user study showed that access to the scalar helps designers select better rewards. The case for a fingerprint is that it should help them repair rewards, so I simulated that workflow. Starting from \(w^{*}\) with one to three dimensions sign-flipped, a simulated designer flips one sign per round under one of three policies: flip the dimension with the most negative fingerprint value, greedily evaluate all eight possible flips and keep the best, or flip at random. Each policy ran for one hundred trials.

On standardized features, the fingerprint policy repairs every reward in exactly as many rounds as there are flipped dimensions, one correct diagnosis per round, succeeding in 100 of 100 trials, while random repair succeeds in 13. The diagnosis itself is clearly sound. The problem is the greedy baseline, which matches the fingerprint round for round at a fraction of the cost, 17 TAC evaluations per trial against roughly 543: with only eight possible edits, simply trying all of them is cheap. On raw features, the fingerprint policy drops to 29 of 100, inheriting the localization failures described earlier, while greedy remains perfect because it directly optimizes the quantity the stopping rule checks. The fingerprint's advantage is architectural: its cost does not grow with the size of the edit vocabulary, since it diagnoses dimensions rather than searching over candidate edits, and in any realistic edit space larger than eight sign flips the greedy baseline stops being runnable. On this benchmark as configured, though, greedy is the better repair policy, and I would rather say that plainly than dress the fingerprint up as a search method. Its value lies in what it tells the designer, not in how it searches.

One last observation from these runs, for which I do not yet have an explanation. On every pool, the Soft-TAC optimizer reaches near-perfect agreement (TAC around 0.99), yet its cosine similarity to \(w^{*}\) ranges from 0.70 to 0.96 across the regenerated pools, and I have not identified what drives the variation. Near-perfect agreement with the preferences is evidently compatible with a wide range of actual recovery, and the scalar cannot indicate where in that range a given run landed.

Discussion

The first TAC paper aims to inform reward designers; the follow-up aims to replace them, learning the reward from preferences and skipping the design step. This work is closer in spirit to the first paper, but its results bear on the second. A learned reward that fits the preferences almost perfectly can still sit anywhere from 0.70 to 0.96 cosine similarity from the intent that generated them, and past the identifiability boundary no optimizer can distinguish a correct reward from a confounded one, because the preferences themselves cannot. Automating reward specification does not remove the practitioner from the loop; it moves them from author to auditor, and an auditor needs instruments that report more than a single number. The fingerprint is a first attempt at such an instrument, validated in a setting where a ground-truth answer key could be constructed.

Summary of results

Supported by the experiments. Exact Shapley attribution of TAC localizes sign-flipped reward dimensions reliably on standardized features, including flips too small for the scalar to register, and the result holds across regenerated trajectory pools, small preference budgets, and uniform label noise. The attribution also corrects a genuine sign confound that univariate screening gets backwards. Detection of a spurious term on a correlated worthless feature fails above a correlation threshold; that threshold rises with the magnitude of the spurious term, and the failure above it is silent rather than loud.

Negative results. The fingerprint is structurally blind to omitted reward terms, and the univariate-gap heuristic for detecting omissions degenerates into a near-constant flag. The label-noise robustness is a \((1 - 2p)\) property of any rank-based method rather than a contribution of this work. In the repair-loop benchmark as configured, exhaustive greedy search is the better repair policy.

Not claimed. Any conclusion about the fingerprints of learned rewards versus their true recovery. That question stays open until the recovery instability across pools (cosine similarity from 0.70 to 0.96 at near-identical TAC) is understood.
